Say "Hey" to DayCay!




PROJECT OVERVIEW

Limited on time at your destination? Don’t know where to go or what to do nearby? DayCay wants build personalized itineraries for you!



THE PROBLEM

Travel services today are designed to help travelers new to the area identify individual activities within a set location. We have observed that travel services are not tailored to suggest itinerary/day plans based on activity proximity or personalized preferences, which limits travelers’ awareness of what else they would enjoy doing near their destinations.

THE SOLUTION

DayCay; a service that generates
user preference/ location-based itinerary
recommendations to help travelers visiting their destinations for a short period of time experience a full-day plan while not having to travel too far.

RESEARCH

Proto-Persona

Before I could dive into my research, I established the proto-persona displayed above, Daniella Simmons, to identify who I believe I am trying to help. In this case, I believe I am trying to help someone whose plans are affected by situational factors.

From there, I established my project’s User Research Plan to focus my goal, research questions, and interview questions on how a user plans their vacation by the day and how they respond to dynamic, situational changes when creating their day plans.

Five people were involved in the interview process:

  • Two females and three males.
  • The interviewees’ ages ranged from 21-60 years old.
  • Each interviewee has been selected based on their range of experience with traveling and planning trips.

Once I transcribed the interview audio to a spreadsheet, I was able to compile the following affinity chart to identify key groupings when it came to planning a trip and existing pain points when it came to locating and shifting overall day travel plans:

DEFINE

After assessing my research further, the concepts of time required to experience places within a day and the user’s location proximity to the beginning and/or end location were identified as the factors I wanted to help users with when planning a day trip; especially since users will almost always establish their day itineraries based on those factors regardless of the type of trip they’re taking. From there, I was able to identify the following User Insight Statement and Problem Statement:

USER INSIGHT STATEMENT:

Liam needs a way to easily assess his itinerary activity options based on location proximity because of his limited time at each destination.

PROBLEM STATEMENT:

I am Liam Kingston, a traveler who has a limited amount of time at my destination, trying to plan a full day of activities that are close by, but I don’t feel like I can see much because most of the recommended activities I want to see are farther away than I thought, which leaves me feeling disappointed.

IDEATE

The next step was to brainstorm potential solutions to the problem of easily planning a trip based on the user’s limited amount of time at their destination and proximity to key activities that they might enjoy. In order to do so, I conducted an “I Like, I Wish, What If” board and narrowed down my solutions via dot voting and a Feature Prioritization Matrix:

Based on the matrix prioritization (especially identifying the ones that were high impact and low complexity), my goal was to focus on a solution that generated alternative itineraries based on activities near destination stops; and from there, “DayCay” was born:


VALUE PROPOSITION:

My organization, DayCay, is developing a service that generates user preference/location based itinerary recommendations to help travelers visiting their destinations for a short period of time experience a full day plan while not having to travel too far.

DayCay will allow users to determine what kind of activities they want to embark on during the day and provide our customers with alternative itinerary overviews based on desired starting locations and itinerary goals.

    TEST

    The next step was to conduct a usability test on my existing user flow/prototype in order to determine if my user could successfully complete the tasks at hand and how long it would take them to compete said tasks. As I tested each user, I would note down the feedback they mentioned or observations I noticed (such as moments of confusion and of confidence).

    After reviewing my notes and the overall feedback I received during the tests, I was able to group them into the follow categories and rank them from a scale of low to high importance (for iteration/improvement):

    Handwritten Usability Test Notes/Observations and User Testing Analysis

    Based on said groupings, I was able to build the following Application/User Priority Matrix to determine what features/pain points to improve upon during the next iteration. More specifically, my goal was to determine what a high priority for DayCay AND the user would be:

    Usability Test Priority Matrix/Analysis


    Based on this analysis, I was able to bring over the following points of improvement to the digital drawing board:


    Order of User Preference Question: During the usability tests, it was noted that there was confusion when answering the Proximity question; "does that (the proximity question) relate to the first question (Select your Location) or the last question (Starting Activity) question?"
    To reduce the confusion, the questions need to be rearranged/rephrased to make it more clear that the Proximity question relates to the Starting Activity that was selected in relation to the destination.

    As part of the iteration process, the following updates were made to the User Preference questionnaire workflow:

    • The Activity question was moved from Question #5 to Question #2, directly after the Location Selection question. This will help to make it more clear that the Proximity question specifically relates to the Activity itself and not the destination as a whole. This change also improves the flow of the questions.
    • The Activity question is now required (before it was optional).
    • The Proximity question now includes the terms “starting point” for clarity.



    Save/Unsave Capability: It was also noted and observed that there was confusion when the participants were asked to Save and then Unsave an Itinerary. More specifically, where to Unsave an Itinerary. The concept of a Shopping cart was mention as a way to illustrate what the user would expect for such a feature.

    TAKEAWAYS

    WHAT I'VE LEARNT:

    • Planning with accessibility and testing with users of all ages and experiences can truly make an impact on your final product.
    • At the beginning, I thought I’d take more situational aspects into account and could fit many ideas into one case study, but you’ve got to start somewhere; start with one big pain point that you can help users with, and expand from there wherever needed.
    • The beginning assumptions may not always be the same as the end result. Users are the key to making that determination.


    POTENTIAL IMPROVEMENTS FOR FUTURE ITERATIONS:

    Although I focused on proximity when it came to location, I think the application itself could also focus on these other topics, if provided the time and resources:

    • A question specifying how many hours you’d like to spend touring within the day (in this case study, I left that up to the user based on proximity).
    • A feature that highlighted weather as a question or a filter.
    • An emphasis that this app doesn’t only have to be AI or manually self-created, but users CAN view the itineraries of other users, rate them, comment on them, and so forth.
    • Word of mouth was a huge factor that was mentioned during user interviews that didn’t get the chance to shine (yet).
